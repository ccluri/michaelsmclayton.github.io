MICHAEL S. CLAYTON (PhD)

neuroscientist, psychologist, & programmer

ABOUT ME

I was born in southern England in November 1990. After receiving a BSc in Psychology from the University of Exeter in 2014, I completed a PhD in Experimental Psychology at the University of Oxford.
During my doctoral research, I focused on how sustained visual attention might be supported by oscillations in human brain activity. Specifically, I investigated the ways in which visual attention might be supported by alpha oscillations (7-13 Hz) in visual regions of the brain. I did this by delivering alternating electrical currents to posterior cortex at various frequencies, and observing the effects this electrical stimulation on visual attention task performance. The results of this research have been published in the Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, and Frontiers in Neuroscience
After submitted my PhD thesis, I worked as a Science Intern for Cambridge Cognition - a digital neuroscience company which develops cognitive tests for use in pharmaceutical trials. Here, I learned about software is developed in commercial environments (e.g. version control, development life cycles), and how to run cognitive psychology experiments using online platforms (namely, Mechanical Turk and Prolific Academic).

INTERESTS

VISUAL PERCEPTION AND ATTENTION

My PhD research in sustained concentration taught me a lot about psychological theories of cognitive control, as well as the neuroscience of how distributed, functional brain networks facilitate successful engagement of such control (e.g. cingular-opercular, fronto-parietal networks). More specifically though, my research has focused on the importance of neural oscillations in visual attention, both in the effective communication of signals from anterior brain regions to sensory areas (see my review paper in Trends in Cognitive Sciences), as well fine-grained temporal control of perceptual processes (see my review paper in the European Journal of Neuroscience).

NEUROIMAGING AND BRAIN STIMULATION

I used electroencephalography (EEG) to record oscillations in human brain activity. I analysed the frequency and phase information contained within this activity using techniques described in this great book! In order to manipulate brain activity, I used transcranial alternating current stimulation (see here for a description). I also have experience with eye-tracking and transcranial magnetic stimulation

PROGRAMMING LANGUAGES

In addition to psychology and neuroscience, I have a strong interest in programming languages. During my PhD, I used MATLAB extensively for stimulus design and presentation (using Psychtoolbox). I also used MATLAB for my analyses of behavioural and neural oscillation data (using libraries like EEGLAB and Fieldtrip). I have commonly used SPSS, but have more recently transferred entirely to R for my statistical analyses.
As well as traditional cognitive testing, I am also very interested in the possibility of running cognitive and perceptual experiments online. To develop these online experiments, I have therefore learned front-end web development languages like HTML, CSS, and Javascript. During my internship at Cambridge Cognition, I used these languages (as well as Javascript libraries like React) to develop cognitive tasks that are suitable for online use. In addition though, I also learned how to use languages like NodeJS for back-end programming, Git for version control, and Bash/Unix scripting.
Most recently, I have also been learning Python for the purposes of running machine learning algorithms (using libraries like TensorFlow). I am particularly interesting in using convolutional neural networks to better understand how the brain categorises visual inputs.
